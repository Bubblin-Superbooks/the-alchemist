humour, according to Jonson, was a bias of disposition, a warp, so to speak, in character by which

"Some one peculiar quality
 Doth so possess a man, that it doth draw
 All his affects, his spirits, and his powers,
 In their confluctions, all to run one way."

But continuing, Jonson is careful to add:

"But that a rook by wearing a pied feather,
 The cable hat-band, or the three-piled ruff,
 A yard of shoe-tie, or the Switzers knot
 On his French garters, should affect a humour!
 O, it is more than most ridiculous."

Jonson's comedy of humours, in a word, conceived of stage personages on the basis of a ruling trait or passion (a notable simplification of actual life be it observed in passing); and, placing these typified traits in juxtaposition in their conflict and contrast, struck the spark of comedy.
